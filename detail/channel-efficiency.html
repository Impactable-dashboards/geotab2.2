Read this first

This is a pipeline-anchored efficiency diagnostic, not a channel beauty contest. The honest headline is that the program is engaging the right named accounts and they are now in late-stage pipeline, but the proof that connects marketing spend to that pipeline exists in only one place: the manually maintained ABM tracker. Everywhere else, the loop is broken by the reseller sales model and by four channels that each count a different conversion event. Closing that loop is the single highest-leverage move available before renewal, and it is the spine of the recommendation in Section 11.

How to read these side by side

Read each channel down its own column, not across to its neighbor. LinkedIn's job is to put the program in front of the right named accounts repeatedly so that when a BDE calls or a Google search happens, the name is already familiar. Its 25 hard conversions undercount its real contribution because most of its value shows up as influence on pipeline that closes through other paths. Google's job is to catch the hand that is already raised. Programmatic keeps frequency up cheaply. Each is doing a different job, and the cost-per-conversion numbers reflect the job, not the quality.

05The Conversion Definition Problem
Before anyone reallocates a dollar based on cost per conversion, this has to be on the table. The four channels are not measuring the same thing, so their efficiency numbers cannot be ranked against each other.

The trap to avoid in the room

Put plainly: a reasonable person looking at the scorecard would conclude LinkedIn is 25 times more expensive than Programmatic and 7 times more expensive than Google. That conclusion is false, and it is false because of measurement, not performance. Programmatic's $98 counts cheap view-through pixels. Google's $321 counts genuine form fills. LinkedIn's $2,458 counts only the small slice of named-account engagement that hard-converts on click, and ignores the influence that shows up in the $938K of named pipeline.

Until all four channels report against one shared definition of a qualified event, any cross-channel efficiency ranking is misleading. Normalizing the definition is recommendation three in Section 11, and it is a prerequisite for any defensible budget shift.

Sizing the gap

Outside the nine hand-tracked ABM accounts, essentially none of Geotab's pipeline is system-attributed to a channel. Google sends 225 form fills a year into a reseller-influenced sales motion with no closed loop back to revenue. LinkedIn's influence on the $938K named pipeline is real but unprovable in a system today. This is the largest measurement liability in the account, and it is the reason a skeptical new leader could look at LinkedIn's 25 conversions and undervalue the program.

The fix is not more reporting on top of broken plumbing. It is the plumbing itself: a person-level visitor identity layer, a revenue-attribution connection into the sales system, and a single dashboard that unifies all four channels. That is Layer 0 in the recommendation, and it is what turns this report from a renewal risk into an expansion case.

07Budget Change & Pacing
Where spend moved this year and what it appears to have done. Budget changes typically take 30 to 90 days to show in pipeline, so treat these as directional.

Google Ads Monthly: Spend vs Conversions

Google is winding down, and that is a bigger deal than it looks

Google spend fell from $16.4K in January to $7.2K in June, with conversions tracking down from 68 to 20. Because Google is 46% of NA spend and, in enterprise, functions as the top and mid-funnel demand-creation engine that LinkedIn then converts, a steady decline does not just trim capture. It shrinks the pool of search-introduced demand the rest of the program relies on to produce pipeline. Confirm with Diego and Julian whether this is a deliberate reallocation or unmanaged drift.

LinkedIn and Programmatic pacing

LinkedIn ran lumpy: a March with two segment blocks at zero conversions, then an April spike (two blocks near $8.3K). Programmatic conversions looked cheapest early ($31 in January) and normalized upward through April, which is consistent with loose early pixel counting rather than a real efficiency cliff.

08Funnel Stage Allocation
Where the money sits by funnel role, corrected for how search behaves in enterprise. Google is not bottom-funnel capture in a long B2B committee cycle. Non-branded category search is first-touch discovery, the re-search a buyer does mid-evaluation is mid-funnel reinforcement, and only branded search is true bottom funnel. LinkedIn then sits mid and bottom funnel as the layer that qualifies the committee and converts the demand search introduced.
The channels are a sequence, not competitors

Google opens and sustains the conversation across top and middle funnel. LinkedIn qualifies the committee and converts the named accounts. Programmatic keeps mid-funnel frequency up. Meta is a light bottom-funnel test. Read this way, LinkedIn's low last-click conversion count is exactly what you would expect from the closing layer. It is converting demand that search introduced, and that value shows up in the $938K of named pipeline, not in last-click.

This is not a barbell

Read with search in its real enterprise role, the program is mid-funnel weighted, which is where enterprise demand is nurtured and qualified, with a healthy discovery layer on top. The earlier single-bucket read that called Google bottom-funnel capture made the allocation look top and bottom heavy and made LinkedIn look expensive. Corrected, the picture is a working sequence: search creates and sustains demand, LinkedIn qualifies and converts it, and the cost-per-conversion differences between them are a function of the stage each plays, not of one channel being better than another.

Funnel shift implied: widen the mid-funnel so LinkedIn can qualify and convert more of the demand search introduces, and protect Google's discovery role rather than treating it as capture to be trimmed. The conversion load belongs on a healthy, well-fed mid-funnel.

09Saturation, Penetration & Frequency
Real platform data from the tracker. Frequency is the average number of times a member saw the ads in the period. Penetration is the share of the matched target audience actually reached. Healthy LinkedIn frequency runs about 2 to 5 times per four weeks. Above 5 to 6, fatigue and diminishing returns set in. Low penetration paired with high frequency means the budget is hitting a narrow pocket over and over.

What the saturation data says

The two largest mid-funnel segments crossed 5x frequency in April (5.2 and 5.45) while reaching only 6 to 9 percent of the audience. That is the narrow-audience saturation trap. Adding budget here buys more repetition, not more reach. The fix is a wider mid-funnel audience, which raises penetration and lowers frequency at the same spend.

The small Segment E hit 12.4x frequency in April on 4 percent penetration. That is over-exposed and wasteful. Cap frequency, refresh creative, or fold it into the unified pool.

Aircover and Segment D sit at low penetration (3 to 4 percent) with healthy frequency. They have reach headroom, though aircover converts through influence, not last click.

Budget shifts this points to

Channel shift. Hold or trim the saturated narrow LinkedIn segments. Redirect into a wider unified mid-funnel retargeting pool across LinkedIn and Programmatic to raise penetration. Do not add frequency to any pocket already above 5x. Add reach instead.

Funnel shift. The mid-funnel is where the program already concentrates, and it is saturating on a narrow audience. Widen it so LinkedIn can qualify and convert more of the demand search introduces. This is the unified retargeting layer the recommendations call Layer 1, now justified by the frequency and penetration data. Protect Google's discovery role; the bigger risk is the opposite, that the demand-creation engine is shrinking (see Pacing).

11Recommendations
Seven moves, ordered. The first three build the measurement spine and the shared definition that everything else depends on. The budget does not need to grow to do this. It needs to be re-sequenced, funding the spine by trimming the loosest-attribution spend.
1

Stand up the measurement spine first Layer 0

Person-level website visitor identity (WebID) to name the decision-makers behind the traffic, the entry source, and the page paths. This is the foundation every other recommendation leans on. Fund it by trimming 10 to 15% from the loosest-attribution spend, not by asking for new budget.

Traces to: Section 06 attribution gap

2

Connect revenue attribution into the sales system Layer 0

Explore the newly available DemandSense revenue-attribution module that ties marketing touches to Salesforce pipeline and revenue. This is the closed loop that does not exist today. Pair it with DemandSense Ad Connections, which already unifies LinkedIn, Google, Meta, and Programmatic in one dashboard, so Google becomes visible without Impactable having to manage it. Add server-side conversion tracking (CAPI) to harden the signal.

Traces to: Section 06 dark tier, Section 03 Google visibility

3

Normalize the conversion definition across all four channels Layer 0

Agree one shared definition of a qualified event and report every channel against it. Until this exists, no cross-channel budget verdict is defensible. This is the prerequisite for any reallocation, and it neutralizes the trap in Section 05.

Traces to: Section 05 definition problem

4

Confirm Google and Meta ownership and the Google wind-down Gate

Establish who runs Google and Meta and on what conversion definitions, and whether the Google decline from $16.4K to $7.2K is intentional. Forty-six percent of NA spend cannot sit in a blind spot. One short note to Diego and Julian closes this.

Traces to: Section 07 pacing

5

Build one unified always-on retargeting layer before fragmenting Layer 1

Mature a single mid and bottom-funnel retargeting motion across the whole ICP first, then split by persona only where a warm pocket has enough audience size to justify it. Premature segmentation is what made the inherited structure read as overly tactical.

Traces to: Section 04 channel roles

6

Keep each channel in its lane Layer 1

Treat LinkedIn as the orchestration and influence layer measured on pipeline contribution, Google as last-click capture, Programmatic as cheap frequency re-tasked to warm audiences, and Meta as a decide-or-fold test. Stop comparing them on a single cost-per-conversion line.

Traces to: Section 04, Section 05

7

Hold top-funnel awareness allocation until the spine produces data Layer 3

Decisions about adding awareness spend by segment and geography should be the last move, made on evidence from the measurement spine rather than instinct. This is where the back-half blank slate gets filled, and it is the natural lead-in to the Impact Report.

Traces to: Section 01 thesis, full report

8

Cap frequency on saturated segments and add reach, not repetition Layer 1

The two largest mid-funnel segments crossed 5x frequency in April on 6 to 9 percent penetration, and one small segment hit 12x. Cap frequency, refresh creative, and widen the audience so the same spend reaches more of the target rather than fatiguing a narrow pocket.

Traces to: Section 09 saturation

9

Strengthen the qualify-and-convert layer and widen the mid-funnel Layer 1

Read in its real enterprise role the funnel is mid-weighted, not a barbell, and the mid-funnel is saturating on a narrow audience. Widen the unified mid-funnel pool so LinkedIn can qualify and convert more of the demand that search introduces, and protect Google's discovery role rather than cutting it. The conversion load belongs on a healthy, well-fed mid-funnel.

Traces to: Section 08 funnel allocation, Section 09 saturation

10

Tag campaigns by funnel stage and wire the ad-data connector Layer 0

The funnel split here is mapped, not measured, because campaigns are not tagged by stage and 1:1 spend is buried in the persona blocks. Tag every campaign TOF, MOF, or BOF, and connect DemandSense so funnel spend, frequency, and penetration are pulled live rather than reconciled by hand.
